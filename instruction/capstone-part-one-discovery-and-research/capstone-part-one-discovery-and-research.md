The capstone project represents the synthesis of everything you have learned about Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) and web design. Up to this point, you have explored the theoretical underpinnings of mental models, the precision of Nielsen’s heuristics, and the technical requirements of responsive design. Now, you transition from student to practitioner. The first phase of this journey is not about writing code or even sketching layouts; it is about discovery. This phase requires you to step back from the "how" of development and focus entirely on the "why" and the "who."


In the industry, this is often referred to as the Discovery Phase. It is the period where you validate your assumptions and ensure that the product you intend to build actually solves a real human problem. Building a technically perfect website that serves no purpose is a failure of design. By the end of this section, you will have defined a clear problem statement and gathered the foundational research necessary to guide your design decisions in the coming weeks.

## Defining the Problem Statement

Every great digital product begins with a problem that needs solving. A common mistake for beginning developers is to start with a solution—such as "I want to build a fitness app"—rather than a problem. To create a successful capstone, you must identify a gap in the current digital landscape or a friction point in a specific user’s daily life.

A strong problem statement is grounded in reality. It should be specific enough to provide direction but broad enough to allow for creative exploration. A helpful framework to use is the "How Might We" (HMW) approach, popularized by design firms like IDEO. Instead of stating a feature, frame it as a challenge: "How might we help local community gardeners coordinate tool sharing to reduce individual costs and storage issues?"

When crafting your problem statement, consider the following criteria:
*   **Feasibility:** Can this be addressed through a web-based solution within the timeframe of this course?
*   **Significance:** Does this problem affect a specific group of people in a meaningful way?
*   **Clarity:** Is the problem stated without suggesting a specific technical implementation?

## Conducting Initial User Research

Once you have a working problem statement, you must test your assumptions. You are not the user, even if you are building something for a demographic you belong to. User research is the process of gathering evidence to support your design choices. For this capstone, your research should focus on understanding the behaviors, motivations, and pain points of your target audience.

The most effective tool at this stage is the semi-structured interview. By speaking with three to five potential users, you can uncover insights that a survey might miss. Focus your questions on past behavior rather than future predictions. For example, instead of asking "Would you use an app for tool sharing?", ask "Tell me about the last time you needed a tool you didn't own. How did you handle that situation?" This reveals the actual hurdles users face, such as the social anxiety of asking a neighbor or the high cost of a one-time rental.

In addition to interviews, consider "desk research." Look for existing data, forum discussions (like Reddit or specialized communities), and reviews of existing products. This helps you understand the language users use and the common complaints they have about current solutions.

## Competitive Audit and Market Landscape

You are rarely solving a problem that has never been addressed before. Even if there isn't a direct competitor, users are likely using "workarounds." If you are building a tool-sharing app, your competitors aren't just other apps; they are hardware stores, neighborhood Facebook groups, and physical bulletin boards.

Conducting a competitive audit involves identifying these direct and indirect competitors and analyzing them through an HCI lens. Use the principles we have studied, such as usability heuristics and accessibility standards, to evaluate where these current solutions fail. Does a competitor have a confusing navigation structure? Is their mobile experience non-existent? Finding these weaknesses allows you to position your capstone project as a superior alternative.

## Identifying Your Target Audience

A website built for everyone is a website built for no one. In this phase, you must narrow your focus to a primary user group. While you may eventually create full personas in the design phase, your research should now focus on identifying "User Goals."

What is the primary objective of your user? A student looking for a study partner has a different goal than a professor looking for a research assistant. By defining these goals early, you create a "north star" for your project. Every feature you propose later must directly contribute to helping the user achieve these goals. If a feature doesn't serve the goal, it is likely "feature creep" and should be discarded.

## Common Challenges in the Discovery Phase

The discovery phase can often feel "messy" because it lacks the immediate gratification of seeing a page render in a browser. One of the most common challenges is **Confirmation Bias**. This occurs when a designer only looks for research that supports their initial idea. To combat this, actively look for reasons why your idea might *not* work. If every interviewee says they prefer buying tools to sharing them, you must be willing to pivot your problem statement.

Another challenge is **Scope Creep**. During research, you might discover ten different problems your users face. It is tempting to try to solve all of them. However, for a capstone project, it is better to solve one problem elegantly than to solve five problems poorly. Use your research to prioritize the "Must-Haves" versus the "Nice-to-Haves."

## Practical Example: From Observation to Research

Imagine a student who notices that their peers struggle to find affordable, healthy meals on campus after 8:00 PM. 

1.  **Initial Idea:** "I'll build a food delivery app for the campus."
2.  **Refined Problem Statement:** "How might we help evening-shift students access healthy, affordable food options when most campus dining halls are closed?"
3.  **Research:** The student interviews four night-shift students. They discover the problem isn't just "food delivery" (which exists via large apps), but the *cost* and *lack of healthy options*.
4.  **Discovery Outcome:** The project shifts from a generic delivery app to a "Late-Night Healthy Meal Tracker" that aggregates vending machine inventory and local deli specials specifically for students on a budget.

This shift, driven by research, ensures the final project is grounded in a real user need rather than a generic assumption.

## Summary

The Discovery and Research phase is the foundation of your capstone project. By moving from a vague idea to a concrete, researched problem statement, you ensure that your work has purpose and direction. You have identified who your users are, what challenges they face, and how existing solutions fall short. 

As you move forward into the design and wireframing phase, keep your research notes close. Every button you place and every navigation link you create should be a response to the insights you gathered during this phase. Remember the words of HCI pioneer Alan Cooper: "If we design for everyone, we design for no one." Focus on your specific user, solve their specific problem, and the technical implementation will follow a much clearer path.
